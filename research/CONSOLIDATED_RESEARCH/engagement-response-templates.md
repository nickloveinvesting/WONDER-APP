# Engagement Response Templates for ARGUED

## How to Use This Guide

This document provides templates and strategies for responding to different types of engagement during promotion and community building. Templates are starting points - adapt to context and maintain genuine conversation.

**Core Principles**:
- Respond quickly but thoughtfully (1-2 hours during active hours)
- Be genuine, not scripted
- Acknowledge valid concerns
- Thank people for engaging (even critics)
- Convert interest to action
- Build relationships, not just signups

## Genuine Interest

### Signal: Someone is interested but needs more information or encouragement to try

**Indicators**:
- Asking specific questions
- "This sounds interesting but..."
- "How would I use this for..."
- "I've been looking for something like this"
- Asking about specific features

### Response Strategy

**Goal**: Convert interest to signup/trial with low-pressure invitation

**Template 1 - Answer question + gentle invitation**:
```
[Answer their specific question clearly]

If you want to try it out, [specific action they can take]. Would love to hear what you think - the platform is really shaped by early user feedback.

[If relevant: Here's a good starting discussion: [link]]
```

**Template 2 - Address concern + personal invitation**:
```
[Address their specific concern]

I think it could work well for [their use case]. Happy to help you get started if you want to try - just [specific action]. Or feel free to poke around public discussions first to get a sense of it: [link]
```

**Template 3 - Validate interest + specific next step**:
```
Glad this resonates! [Brief answer to their question]

Easy way to start: [specific, low-commitment action]
- Browse public discussions: [link]
- Join [specific discussion relevant to their interest]
- Start a discussion about [their mentioned interest]

Let me know if you have questions!
```

### Examples

**Scenario: "This sounds interesting, but I'm not sure how I'd use it."**

Good Response:
```
Fair question! A few common starting points:
- If you're reading a philosophy book and want to discuss it, start/join a reading group
- If you have a philosophical question you're curious about, post it and see who engages
- If you're taking a philosophy class, use it for extended discussions beyond class time

What kind of philosophical engagement are you most interested in? That might help narrow down how you'd use it.

Feel free to browse public discussions to get a sense: [link]
```

Why it works: Gives concrete use cases, asks clarifying question to personalize, provides action without pressure.

---

**Scenario: "I've been wanting something like this for my reading group!"**

Good Response:
```
That's exactly one of the main use cases! The reading group features let you:
- Post discussion questions about specific sections/chapters
- Reference quotes and passages easily
- Have threaded conversations that persist over weeks
- Track who's read what

Would love to have your group try it out. You can create a private reading group (just your members) or public (open to others). Here's how to start: [link to guide]

Happy to help you get set up if you want - just let me know!
```

Why it works: Shows you understand their need, specific features for their use case, clear next steps, offer to help.

---

**Scenario: "How is this different from [competitor]?"**

Good Response:
```
[Competitor] is good for [their strength]. Main differences for us:
- [Specific difference 1]
- [Specific difference 2]
- [Specific difference 3]

We're more [your positioning] vs their [their positioning].

If [competitor] works for you, great! This is really for [specific use case].

Want to see examples? Here are a few discussions that showcase what we do differently: [link]
```

Why it works: Respects competitor, specific differences, shows examples, no pressure.

## Criticism & Skepticism

### Signal: Someone is skeptical or critical, but potentially persuadable

**Indicators**:
- "Another X? No thanks"
- "This won't work because..."
- "I don't see why we need this"
- Pointing out potential problems
- Comparing unfavorably to existing solutions

### Response Strategy

**Goal**: Acknowledge concern, provide perspective, remain open to being wrong

**Template 1 - Acknowledge validity + your approach**:
```
[Acknowledge their concern is valid/understandable]

[Explain your specific approach to addressing it]

You're right that [potential failure mode]. [How you're trying to avoid it]

Not claiming we have perfect solution, but if you've felt [problem you're solving], worth checking out. No pressure if you're skeptical.
```

**Template 2 - Concede partially + narrow value prop**:
```
You're right about [the thing they're right about].

We're not trying to [what they think you're trying to do]. The specific problem we're solving is [narrow problem].

If you haven't felt that problem, you don't need this. But if you have, [value proposition].
```

**Template 3 - Turn criticism into collaboration**:
```
This is a fair concern. [Acknowledge specifically]

How would you solve [their concern]? Genuinely interested - we're building this with community input and this kind of feedback is valuable.

[If they're interested: We're trying [your approach]. Would love your thoughts on whether that makes sense.]
```

### Examples

**Scenario: "Another social network? Hard pass."**

Good Response:
```
I totally get the platform fatigue. Honest answer: this isn't really a social network in the Twitter/Facebook sense. No feeds, no follower counts, no "staying active" pressure.

It's more like infrastructure for specific conversations - think of it as "tool you use when you need it" rather than "network you maintain."

That said, if you're at capacity with platforms, I understand. The offer stands if you ever specifically need a tool for philosophical discussions or reading groups.
```

Why it works: Validates their fatigue, clarifies misconception, respects their decision, leaves door open.

---

**Scenario: "Will anyone actually use this? Chicken-egg problem is real."**

Good Response:
```
You're absolutely right - it's early. About [X] users currently, which is small.

Here's how I think about it: Even with 50 people having good conversations, it's useful. This isn't Instagram where you need millions. Philosophy discussions can be valuable with dozens of engaged people.

That said, I'm actively looking for people who want to help build this - early users literally shape what it becomes. If you want to wait until it's bigger, totally understand. If you want to help shape it early, would love to have you.
```

Why it works: Honest about numbers, reframes expectation, gives two valid options, respects their concern.

---

**Scenario: "This looks like you're trying to gamify philosophy. Hard no."**

Good Response:
```
I completely agree that would be terrible. Good news: we explicitly avoided gamification.

No likes, no follower counts, no points, no leaderboards, no engagement algorithms. The "tech" here is just infrastructure: better threading for complex arguments, tools for reading groups, optional debate structure.

If you see specific features that feel gamified, I'd genuinely want to know - that's the opposite of what we're going for.

Does that address your concern, or is there something specific that looks like gamification to you?
```

Why it works: Agrees with their values, specific about what you're NOT doing, invites specific feedback, genuine question.

---

**Scenario: "Philosophy doesn't need this. Just read books and talk to people."**

Good Response:
```
Absolutely agree that's the core of philosophy. This isn't trying to replace that.

It's for the practical reality that the people you want to talk to about philosophy might not be in the same city, or you want to discuss a specific text but don't have a local reading group, or you had a great conversation on Twitter that you wish could continue properly.

Philosophy itself doesn't need tech. But people doing philosophy sometimes need better tools than Twitter threads or Reddit comments. If you don't feel that need, you don't need this.
```

Why it works: Agrees with core point, explains practical problem, respects their position.

## Feature Requests

### Signal: Someone wants a feature you don't have

**Indicators**:
- "Does this have X feature?"
- "I wish you had..."
- "Would be great if..."
- "Deal-breaker if you don't have..."

### Response Strategy

**Goal**: Acknowledge request, be honest about status, track for future

**Template 1 - Feature exists**:
```
Yes! [Feature] works like this: [explanation]

[If not obvious: Here's how to access it: [instructions]]

Let me know if you try it and have feedback on how it works.
```

**Template 2 - Feature doesn't exist but is planned**:
```
Great question - this is on the roadmap for [timeframe/priority level].

Currently you can [workaround if any], but proper [feature] support is coming.

Mind if I follow up when we ship this? Would love your feedback on the implementation. [Or: What's your specific use case for this? Helps us prioritize and build it right.]
```

**Template 3 - Feature doesn't exist and might not**:
```
We don't have this currently. [Brief explanation of why, if relevant]

This is the first I've heard this requested - can you tell me more about your use case? If this is important for several people, we could prioritize it.

[If applicable: In the meantime, here's a possible workaround: [workaround]]
```

**Template 4 - Feature request conflicts with philosophy**:
```
Interesting idea! We've actually intentionally avoided [feature] because [reason aligned with platform philosophy].

The concern is [potential problem]. That said, I could see how [their use case] would benefit.

How would you address [the concern]? Maybe there's a way to get the benefit without the downside.
```

### Examples

**Scenario: "Can I use this on mobile?"**

Good Response:
```
Yes - it's web-based so works in mobile browsers. That said, the mobile experience isn't fully optimized yet (works better on tablet/desktop).

Native mobile apps are on the roadmap for [timeframe]. Current mobile web is functional but not ideal for long-form reading/writing.

If you're primarily mobile-only, worth trying to see if it works for your needs, but heads up that desktop is currently a better experience.
```

---

**Scenario: "Does this integrate with [tool/platform]?"**

Good Response:
```
Not yet, but this is a good idea. What's your use case - what would you want the integration to do?

If several people need this, we could prioritize it.

[If there's a workaround: In the meantime, you can [workaround].]

Mind if I add you to a list of people interested in this and follow up when we explore it?
```

---

**Scenario: "I want to follow specific users like Twitter."**

Good Response (if you don't want this feature):
```
Interesting! We've actually intentionally avoided traditional "follow" mechanics because we're trying to focus on ideas/discussions rather than personality-based following.

You can bookmark discussions you're interested in and join reading groups with people whose thinking you appreciate.

That said, several people have asked about this. What's your specific use case? Maybe there's a way to get what you need without full social-follow dynamics.
```

## Comparison Questions

### Signal: Trying to understand positioning relative to known platforms

**Indicators**:
- "How is this different from X?"
- "Isn't this just like X?"
- "Why not use X instead?"
- "X already does this"

### Response Strategy

**Goal**: Differentiate without bashing, position as complementary when possible

**Template 1 - Complementary positioning**:
```
[Competitor] is great for [their strength]. We're focused on [your different focus].

The key differences:
- [Difference 1]
- [Difference 2]

Think of them as complementary - [competitor] for [use case], ARGUED for [different use case].

If [competitor] works for you, keep using it! This is for [specific different need].
```

**Template 2 - Different purpose entirely**:
```
[Competitor] and ARGUED are actually pretty different even though [surface similarity].

[Competitor] is designed for [their purpose]
We're designed for [your purpose]

Different tools for different jobs. Here's an example of what we handle well that [competitor] doesn't: [example/link]
```

**Template 3 - Acknowledge overlap + explain differentiation**:
```
There's definitely some overlap! Both [shared feature].

Where we differ:
- [Your approach to X] vs [their approach to X]
- [Feature you have they don't]
- [Different philosophy/focus]

Some people will prefer [competitor]'s approach, some will prefer ours. Depends on your needs.
[If relevant: You can use both for different purposes]
```

### Examples

**Scenario: "Isn't this just like Reddit's philosophy subs?"**

Good Response:
```
There's definitely overlap - both are for philosophy discussions!

Where we differ:
- Reddit threads die after 2-3 days; we're designed for conversations over weeks/months
- We have specific tools for reading groups and structured debates
- Threading is designed for tracking complex philosophical arguments, not just chronological replies
- No karma/voting system optimizing for popular over substantive

Think of it as complementary: Reddit for broad Q&A and community, ARGUED for sustained deep discussions and reading groups.

If Reddit works for you, great! This is for specific use cases Reddit's architecture doesn't handle well.
```

---

**Scenario: "How is this different from Kialo?"**

Good Response:
```
Kialo is great for structured debate mapping - we actually support similar debate structure when you want it.

The key difference: Kialo is debate-focused (always structured), we support multiple formats:
- Debates (structured like Kialo)
- Unstructured conversations (Socratic dialogue, exploration)
- Reading groups (discussing texts together)

So: Use Kialo when you specifically want debate mapping. Use ARGUED when you want flexibility or reading groups.

They're not mutually exclusive - different tools for different conversations.
```

---

**Scenario: "Why not just use Discord?"**

Good Response:
```
Discord is great for philosophy community chat! But it's built for real-time conversation, which creates problems for philosophical discourse:
- Moves too fast for careful thought
- Threading is limited
- Conversations scroll away
- Timezone issues exclude people
- Not designed for long-form arguments

ARGUED is built for async, persistent, long-form discourse.

In practice, many communities use both: Discord for chat and community, ARGUED for sustained discussions and reading groups. Complementary tools.
```

**Indicators**:
- "What's the tech stack?"
- "Is this open source?"
- "Does this work on X?"
- "What about Y technical capability?"
- Privacy/security questions

### Response Strategy

**Goal**: Answer clearly and specifically, be honest about limitations

**Template 1 - Direct answer**:
```
[Direct answer to their question]

[If relevant: Technical details: [details]]

[If there are limitations: Currently [limitation], but [future plan if any]]

Any other technical questions I can answer?
```

**Template 2 - Privacy/security question**:
```
[Specific answer to their concern]

Our approach: [specific policies/practices]

Here's our full [privacy policy/security documentation]: [link]

What specific concerns do you have? Happy to address them.
```

**Template 3 - Feature capability question**:
```
[Yes/no answer]

[How it works: details]

[If limited: Current limitations: [specifics], working on: [improvements]]

Does that cover your use case, or do you need something more specific?
```

### Examples

**Scenario: "Is this open source?"**

Good Response:
```
Not currently. We're exploring open-sourcing parts of it (particularly [specific components]), but the full platform is currently closed source.

Open source is on the roadmap - want to get the architecture right first before open-sourcing and dealing with external contributions.

What's your interest in this? Using it yourself, contributing, transparency, or something else? Helps me understand how to prioritize this.
```

---

Good Response:
```
Good question. Here's our approach:

1. AI features are completely optional (can be disabled entirely)
2. Private discussions stay private - not used for training
3. We use [Anthropic/OpenAI/provider] API with [their privacy policy]
4. You can export all your data anytime
5. Full privacy policy: [link]

Specific concerns I can address? Happy to go into detail on any aspect.
```

---

**Scenario: "Does this work offline?"**

Good Response:
```
Not currently - requires internet connection.

Offline capabilities (draft responses offline, sync later) are on the roadmap but not implemented yet.

Is offline usage important for your use case? Helps us prioritize this feature.
```

## Trolling & Bad Faith

### Signal: Not genuine engagement, trying to provoke

**Indicators**:
- Clearly mocking without engaging with substance
- Personal attacks
- Dismissive without specific criticism
- Bad faith rhetorical questions
- Trying to start flamewars

### Response Strategy

**Goal**: Usually ignore; occasionally respond once then disengage

**Default: Don't Engage**

Most trolling should be ignored. Responding rewards the behavior.

**If You Must Respond (once)**:

**Template 1 - Brief, neutral, then disengage**:
```
[If there's any substance to address: brief answer]

Happy to discuss if you have specific questions or concerns. Otherwise, have a good one!
```

**Template 2 - Acknowledge disagreement, don't engage**:
```
Sounds like this isn't for you - that's fine! Different tools for different people.
```

### Examples

**Scenario: "lol another failed startup. Good luck with that."**

Best Response: Ignore completely.

If you must respond:
```
Thanks for the well-wishes! Have a good one.
```
Then don't engage further.

---

**Scenario: "Philosophy doesn't need tech bros reinventing Reddit badly."**

Option 1 (Usually best): Ignore.

Option 2 (If you respond once):
```
Fair to be skeptical. If you're happy with current tools, great! This is for people who've felt specific frustrations. Not for everyone.
```
Then don't engage further.

---

**Scenario: Bad faith rhetorical question like "So you're just trying to make money off philosophy?"**

Best approach: Ignore.

If you must respond:
```
Business model is still being figured out - exploring options that don't compromise platform mission. Full transparency on that: [link to relevant info].

Happy to discuss specific concerns if you have them.
```

Then disengage if they continue in bad faith.

---

**When to Respond to Apparent Trolling**:
- If there's a genuine question buried in snark (rare)
- If others are watching and your response helps them
- If it's in a formal space (AMA, etc.) where not responding looks bad

**When NOT to Respond**:
- Pure mockery with no substance
- Personal attacks
- Obvious bad faith
- Trying to start an argument
- On their turf (their post mocking you)

**Remember**: Trolls want attention. Don't give it to them.

## Confusion or Misunderstanding

### Signal: They misunderstand what you're building

**Indicators**:
- "So this is [wrong description]?"
- Criticism based on misunderstanding
- Comparing to something very different
- Confused questions

### Response Strategy

**Goal**: Clarify gently without being condescending

**Template 1 - Gentle clarification**:
```
I may not have explained clearly!

It's not quite [what they think]. It's actually [correct description].

[Example to illustrate: Like [analogy/example]]

Does that make more sense?
```

**Template 2 - Acknowledge confusion + explain**:
```
Good question - that part might not be clear from [wherever they saw it].

[Clear explanation of what it actually is]

[If relevant: Here's an example that might help: [link]]

What made you think it was [their misunderstanding]? Helps me know what to clarify in the description.
```

**Template 3 - Turn confusion into improvement**:
```
Ah, I see the confusion - [what caused it].

Actually [correct info]. Let me explain: [clear explanation]

This is helpful feedback - I should make this clearer. Thanks for the question!
```

### Examples

**Scenario: "So this is like a Slack for philosophy?"**

Good Response:
```
Not quite - I can see why it might seem that way though!

It's actually more like persistent, threaded discussions (think Reddit structure) but designed specifically for philosophical discourse. So: long-form arguments, reading groups, sustained conversations over weeks.

Slack is real-time chat; this is async, persistent discourse.

More like "if Reddit and a philosophy seminar had a baby" than "philosophy Slack."

Does that distinction make sense?
```

---

**Scenario: "Wait, is this an AI that argues with you?"**

Good Response:
```
No - good to clarify this!

It's a platform for *human* philosophical discussions. Humans create and participate in all conversations.

AI features are optional tools to *facilitate* human discussion (like summarizing long threads, or helping steelman an argument). But the AI isn't participating - just assisting.

Think of it as: humans having conversations, with optional AI assistance, not AI engaging in philosophy.

Did I describe it somewhere in a confusing way? Want to make sure this is clear!
```

---

**Scenario: Criticism based on misunderstanding, like "I don't want an algorithm telling me what philosophy to read"**

Good Response:
```
Totally agree - that would be terrible!

To clarify: there's no recommendation algorithm or feed. You choose what discussions to join, what to read, what to participate in. No "here's what you should engage with" algorithmic curation.

It's the opposite of algorithmic feeds - you're in full control of what you see and engage with.

Does that address your concern?
```

## Follow-Up Strategies

### When to Follow Up

**DO Follow Up**:
- ✅ Someone expressed interest but didn't sign up
- ✅ Someone requested a feature - when you build it
- ✅ Someone had a concern - when you address it
- ✅ Someone offered to help - to take them up on it
- ✅ Someone asked to be kept updated
- ✅ Someone engaged substantively multiple times

**DON'T Follow Up**:
- ❌ Someone politely declined
- ❌ Clear they're not interested
- ❌ Troll or bad faith actor
- ❌ You already followed up once with no response

### Follow-Up Templates

**Template 1 - Feature they requested is ready**:
```
Hi [name] - you asked about [feature] a while back. Just wanted to let you know we shipped it!

[Brief description of how it works]

Would love your feedback if you try it out. Thanks for the suggestion!

[Link if relevant]
```

**Template 2 - Checking in with interested person**:
```
Hey [name] - you mentioned being interested in [specific thing] when we talked [timeframe] ago.

[Update on that thing / invitation to try it]

No pressure - just thought you might be interested!
```

**Template 3 - Re-engaging advocate**:
```
Hi [name] - thanks again for [what they did].

[Relevant update they'd care about]

Would love your thoughts on [specific thing relevant to them] if you have time.

Thanks for being part of building this!
```

## Response Time Guidelines

**During Active Promotion** (launch week, major post, AMA):
- Respond within 1-2 hours during active hours
- Quick responses to simple questions
- Thoughtful responses to complex questions (take time to get it right)
- Acknowledge criticism within 1-2 hours even if full response takes longer

**Normal Ongoing Engagement**:
- Respond within 24 hours
- Prioritize substantive questions over simple ones
- Quality over speed

**When NOT to Rush Response**:
- Complex criticism that needs thought
- Feature requests that need discussion
- Questions you don't know the answer to (research first)
- Emotionally charged situations (cool down first)

## Engagement Best Practices

### DO:
- ✅ Thank people for engaging (even critics)
- ✅ Acknowledge valid concerns genuinely
- ✅ Admit what you don't know
- ✅ Ask clarifying questions
- ✅ Provide specific examples
- ✅ Track feature requests
- ✅ Follow up when relevant
- ✅ Let community respond too (don't dominate)
- ✅ Be humble about chances of success
- ✅ Focus on problems you're solving
- ✅ Invite collaboration
- ✅ Respond to substance, ignore trolling

### DON'T:
- ❌ Get defensive
- ❌ Argue with trolls
- ❌ Bash competitors
- ❌ Make promises you can't keep
- ❌ Oversell or hype
- ❌ Dismiss concerns
- ❌ Respond when emotional
- ❌ Respond to everything (pick your battles)
- ❌ Sound like a salesperson
- ❌ Dominate the conversation
- ❌ Chase people who aren't interested

### Remember:
- You're building relationships, not just getting signups
- Critics can become advocates if you respond well
- Not everyone needs to be convinced
- Community members can respond too - you don't have to
- Quality of engagement > quantity of responses
- Being genuine > being perfect

## Tracking Engagement

### What to Track

**For Product Development**:
- Feature requests (what + who requested)
- Common objections (patterns in criticism)
- Confusion points (what needs clearer explanation)
- Competitor comparisons (how people position you)

**For Community Building**:

## Platform-Specific Considerations

### Reddit
- **Tone**: More casual, self-deprecating humor okay
- **Detail**: Can be longer/more detailed
- **Timing**: Respond quickly in first hour (visibility)
- **Community**: Let community respond, don't dominate
- **Follow-up**: Edit original post with updates, not multiple comments

### Twitter
- **Tone**: Concise, clear
- **Detail**: Brief, link to more detail
- **Timing**: Fast responses valued
- **Threading**: Use threads for longer responses
- **Follow-up**: Can @ people but don't be pushy

### Hacker News
- **Tone**: Technical, honest about challenges
- **Detail**: Detailed, thoughtful responses valued
- **Timing**: First few hours critical
- **Community**: Highly engaged, technical audience
- **Criticism**: Take seriously, respond substantively

### Philosophy Forums/Subreddits
- **Tone**: Intellectually serious, humble
- **Detail**: Can be very detailed and specific
- **Timing**: Less time-sensitive
- **Community**: Respect existing community norms
- **Expertise**: Don't oversell if you're not a philosopher

### Discord/Slack Communities
- **Tone**: Conversational, casual
- **Detail**: Moderate, can follow up
- **Timing**: Real-time or async depending on community
- **Community**: Build relationships first, promote second
- **Presence**: Join conversations beyond just promoting

## Remember: The Goal

You're not trying to:
- Convert everyone
- Win arguments
- Defend against all criticism
- Get maximum signups

You're trying to:
- Build genuine relationships
- Find people with real needs you can solve
- Get honest feedback to build better
- Create community of engaged users
- Be helpful and authentic

Quality engagement > quantity of responses.

Building something useful > defending something perfect.

Community > customer acquisition.

Be human, be helpful, be honest.

# Optional Debate Mode: When Competitive Debate Fits In

## Executive Summary

Competitive debate has value as a learning tool and skill-building exercise - but it should be ONE optional feature of a philosophy platform, not the centerpiece. This research examines when and how users might want competitive debate judging, how to frame it as an advanced optional feature, and critically, how to prevent debate culture from dominating the platform's broader mission of collaborative philosophical inquiry.

**Key Findings:**
- Debate mode serves specific purposes: **skill development, persuasion practice, and structured argument evaluation**
- Must be **clearly optional and explicitly opted-into** by participants - never default
- Debate judging should have **transparent criteria** that users understand and agree to
- Platform culture matters more than features: design should **actively prevent competitive status-seeking from dominating**
- Debate is most valuable as a **learning tool, not as a status mechanism**

**Core Principle:** Debate mode is a tool for certain contexts, not the primary purpose of philosophical dialogue. Conversation mode is the default; debate mode is the exception.

## Debate Mode vs. Conversation Mode

### Fundamental Differences

**Conversation Mode (Default):**
- **Goal:** Explore questions, deepen understanding, consider multiple perspectives
- **Success:** Participants gain insight, discover new angles, think more deeply
- **AI Role:** Facilitator who asks questions, provides context, connects ideas
- **Tone:** Collaborative inquiry, "thinking together"
- **Outcome:** No winner/loser - multiple perspectives represented
- **Value:** In the process of exploration itself

**Debate Mode (Optional):**
- **Goal:** Persuade audience/judge, defend position, identify strongest argument
- **Success:** Participant makes most compelling case for their position
- **AI Role:** Judge who evaluates arguments against clear criteria
- **Tone:** Competitive advocacy, "making your case"
- **Outcome:** Winner declared based on argument quality
- **Value:** In developing argumentation skills and evaluating strength of positions

**Not Binary Opposition:**
These modes exist on a spectrum. Some discussions may have elements of both. But the default orientation matters enormously for platform culture.

### When Each Mode Is Appropriate

**Conversation Mode Is Best For:**
- Open-ended philosophical questions with no clear answer
- Exploring personal values and ethical questions
- Building understanding across different perspectives
- Learning about unfamiliar philosophical traditions
- Community dialogue and relationship-building
- Most philosophical inquiry most of the time

**Debate Mode Is Best For:**
- Practicing argumentation skills
- Testing strength of specific arguments
- Academic/educational contexts requiring assessment
- Structured competitions or tournaments
- Cases where participants explicitly want competitive evaluation
- Specific, well-defined positions being defended

### The Default Matters

**Why Conversation Mode Should Be Default:**

1. **Philosophy is collaborative inquiry** - Historical model is dialogue (Socrates, Plato) not combat
2. **Most questions don't have "right answers"** - Debate's win/lose framing misrepresents philosophical nature
3. **Status competition discourages participation** - Many avoid philosophy because they fear looking "dumb"
4. **Diverse participation styles** - Not everyone thrives in competitive environments
5. **Exploration requires safety** - Hard to genuinely consider new ideas if you're defending a position for points

**Debate Mode as Opt-In:**
- Must be explicitly chosen by participants
- Clear labeling: "This discussion is in Debate Mode"
- Both/all parties agree to competitive evaluation
- Can switch modes during discussion only with mutual consent

## When Users Might Want Competitive Debate

### Legitimate Use Cases for Debate Mode

**1. Skill Development**
- Users want to improve argumentation abilities
- Practicing for academic debate, law school, etc.
- Structured feedback on argument quality
- Deliberate practice of persuasive techniques

**Context:** Educational, self-improvement focused

**2. Argument Stress-Testing**
- User has developed a position and wants to test its strength
- "Can my argument withstand strong objections?"
- Identifying weaknesses to improve reasoning
- Academic rigor and intellectual honesty

**Context:** Refinement of existing work

**3. Structured Academic Exercises**
- Class assignments requiring formal debate
- Educational institutions using platform for teaching
- Assessment of student learning outcomes
- Practicing specific philosophical methods

**Context:** Pedagogical, instructor-led

**4. Tournament/Competition**
- Users who enjoy competitive intellectual activities
- Platform-hosted debate tournaments
- Fun, game-like engagement with philosophy
- Community-building through friendly competition

**Context:** Optional competitive events

**5. Clear-Cut Advocacy**
- Policy questions where persuasion matters
- Applied ethics cases with practical implications
- Situations where "which argument is stronger?" is genuinely relevant
- Testing arguments that might be used in real-world advocacy

**Context:** Applied philosophy with practical stakes

### Problematic Motivations for Debate Mode

**What We Should NOT Encourage:**

❌ **Status-Seeking** - Using debate victories to claim superiority
❌ **Ego Validation** - Needing AI/platform to declare you "smart"
❌ **Gatekeeping** - "Real philosophers debate rigorously; casual discussion is shallow"
❌ **Competitive Posturing** - More interested in winning than truth-seeking
❌ **Harassment Vector** - Challenging people to debates to humiliate them

**Design Principle:** Structure debate mode to serve learning and genuine argument evaluation, not ego or status.

## How to Frame Debate as Optional Advanced Feature

### Positioning in Platform Hierarchy

**Primary Tier: Philosophical Dialogue** (always visible, default)
- Open conversations
- Collaborative inquiry
- AI facilitation (questions, context, summaries)
- Inclusive, welcoming, exploratory

**Secondary Tier: Structured Discussion Tools** (available but not prominent)
- Argument mapping
- Formal logic tools
- Position development
- Thought experiment frameworks

**Tertiary Tier: Competitive Debate Mode** (clearly marked as specialized tool)
- Opt-in only
- Explicit rules and judging criteria
- Educational/skill-building framing
- Not default or encouraged for typical use

**Visual/UX Implications:**
- Debate mode is a setting you turn on, not a constant option
- "Create Conversation" is big, prominent button
- "Create Debate" is smaller, in advanced options, with explanation
- Onboarding emphasizes conversation, mentions debate as optional advanced feature

### Educational Framing

**Position Debate Mode As:**

✅ **"Advanced Argumentation Workshop"**
- For users who want structured feedback on reasoning
- Skill-building emphasis
- Optional module, not core experience

✅ **"Argument Lab"**
- Experimental space for testing ideas
- Scientific metaphor (lab) not competitive (arena)
- Focus on learning, not winning

✅ **"Structured Evaluation Mode"**
- Clear, transparent assessment against criteria
- Educational utility
- Demystifies what makes arguments strong/weak

❌ **NOT "Battle Arena" or "Philosophy Fight Club"**
- Avoid combat metaphors
- Don't glorify winning
- Don't create culture of philosophical one-upmanship

### Communication About Debate Mode

**In Platform Messaging:**

"Most philosophical conversations thrive as collaborative explorations where multiple perspectives enrich understanding. However, we also offer **Debate Mode** for users who want structured, competitive evaluation of arguments - whether for skill development, academic practice, or testing the strength of specific positions. Debate Mode is entirely optional and must be explicitly chosen by all participants."

**In Onboarding:**

"Welcome to [Platform]! Here, you'll engage in thoughtful philosophical dialogue, exploring questions alongside others who are curious about ideas. Our AI facilitator helps deepen conversations by asking questions, providing context, and connecting related discussions.

For users interested in developing argumentation skills, we also offer optional Debate Mode with AI judging - but most conversations work best as collaborative inquiry rather than competitive debate."

**In FAQ:**

"**When should I use Debate Mode?**
Debate Mode is ideal when you want to practice argumentation, get structured feedback on reasoning, or participate in a friendly competition. It's NOT necessary for most philosophical discussions, which benefit from exploration rather than evaluation. Only use Debate Mode when all participants explicitly agree to competitive judging."

## AI Judging in Debate Mode: Criteria and Transparency

### The Transparency Imperative

**Problem:** Opaque AI judgment creates frustration and distrust

**Solution:** Completely transparent criteria that users see before agreeing to debate

**Before Debate Begins:**
Users see exactly what will be evaluated:
- Clarity of argument structure
- Logical validity
- Evidence and support
- Response to objections
- Consistency
- Philosophical sophistication

**After Debate:**
Users see exactly how they were scored on each criterion, with specific examples from their arguments

### Judging Criteria (Research-Based)

**From Online Debate Platform Research (2024):**

Platforms use AI to assess arguments based on:
- **Logical coherence** - Does the reasoning follow?
- **Evidence support** - Are claims backed up?
- **Clarity** - Is the argument well-structured and understandable?
- **Responsiveness** - Does participant address counterarguments?

**Adapted for Philosophy:**

**1. Argument Structure (25%)**
- Clear claim/conclusion
- Stated premises
- Logical connection between premises and conclusion
- Organized presentation

**2. Philosophical Depth (25%)**
- Engagement with relevant philosophical concepts
- Understanding of theoretical frameworks
- Nuance and sophistication of thinking
- Awareness of implications

**3. Evidence and Reasoning (20%)**
- Support for claims
- Logical validity
- Use of examples or thought experiments
- Philosophical rigor

**4. Engagement with Opposition (15%)**
- Response to counterarguments
- Acknowledgment of objections
- Refinement based on challenges
- Intellectual charity to opposing view

**5. Clarity and Communication (15%)**
- Clear expression of ideas
- Precise use of terms
- Accessible explanation
- Effective rhetoric (non-manipulative)

**Note:** These percentages are illustrative - actual weighting should be transparent and potentially customizable

### Types of Debate Judgments

**1. Holistic Evaluation**
"Based on the arguments presented, [Participant A] made a more compelling case. Key strengths: [X, Y, Z]. Areas for improvement: [A, B]."

**Best for:** General assessment, tournament-style debates

**2. Criteria-Based Scoring**
- Argument Structure: A gets 8/10, B gets 7/10
- Philosophical Depth: A gets 7/10, B gets 9/10
- [etc. for each criterion]
- Total Score: A 78/100, B 81/100

**Best for:** Educational contexts, skill development focus

**3. Comparative Analysis**
"Both participants presented strong arguments:
- [Participant A] excelled at [X] but struggled with [Y]
- [Participant B] excelled at [Z] but struggled with [W]
- On balance, [Winner] because [specific reasoning]"

**Best for:** Learning-focused debates where both sides should improve

**4. Technical Debate Scoring** (Traditional Academic Debate)
- Flow analysis (tracking arguments through rounds)
- Impact weighing (which arguments matter most)
- Dropped arguments (what went unresponded to)
- Traditional debate scoring rubrics

**Best for:** Users familiar with formal debate seeking that specific feedback

**User Choice:** Let participants choose which type of judging they want before debate begins

### Handling Subjectivity Transparently

**Acknowledge Limits:**

"AI debate judging evaluates arguments based on stated criteria, but philosophical questions often don't have objectively 'correct' answers. A debate winner is the participant who made the most effective case *in this context*, not who is 'right' in any absolute sense. Different judges (human or AI) might weigh factors differently."

**Multiple Judge Options:**

1. **AI Judge** - Consistent criteria, instant feedback
2. **Peer Judge** - Community members volunteer to evaluate
3. **Panel** - Multiple judges (AI + humans) for important debates
4. **Audience Vote** - Community votes on winner (though this has problems - popularity ≠ quality)

**Best Practice:** For high-stakes debates, use multiple judges with averaged scores

### Feedback Beyond Win/Loss

**Debate Mode Should Provide:**

✅ **Detailed Reasoning Feedback**
- "Your argument would be stronger if you addressed [objection]"
- "Consider clarifying what you mean by [term]"
- "This premise seems to contradict your earlier point about [X]"

✅ **Philosophical Development Suggestions**
- "Explore [relevant philosophical tradition] to deepen this argument"
- "Your reasoning here resembles [philosopher]'s position - reading their work might help"
- "Consider the distinction between [X] and [Y] to refine your claim"

✅ **Comparison to Strong Arguments**
- "Effective arguments on this topic typically include [features]"
- "Similar debates have hinged on [key question] - did you address this?"

❌ **Not Just "You Lost"**
- Even losing debates should provide actionable learning
- Focus on growth, not just judgment
- Celebrate good reasoning even in losing argument

## Preventing Debate Culture from Dominating Platform

### The Cultural Risk

**Problem:** Competitive debate culture can overwhelm collaborative inquiry

**Historical Examples:**
- Online forums where "destroying" opponents becomes the norm
- Philosophy communities becoming hostile to newcomers
- Status hierarchies based on debate victories
- "Debate me!" becoming weapon of harassment

**Prevention is Essential:** Platform design must actively prevent these dynamics

### Design Mechanisms to Protect Conversation Culture

**1. Default to Conversation Mode**
- All new discussions are conversational by default
- Requires explicit action to enable debate mode
- Onboarding emphasizes collaborative inquiry

**2. Limit Debate Mode Visibility**
- Debate victories don't appear on user profiles prominently
- No "Top Debater" leaderboards
- No debate ranking system visible to others
- Keep debate achievements private or minimally visible

**3. Prevent Unsolicited Debate Challenges**
- Can't challenge someone to debate without their consent
- No "Debate me!" harassment vector
- Both parties must explicitly agree
- Can decline without social penalty

**4. Separate Spaces**
- Debate discussions clearly labeled
- Filter option to hide all debate-mode content
- Dedicated debate tournaments in separate section
- Conversation spaces protected from debate culture

**5. Moderate Language and Tone**
- Community guidelines emphasize respectful engagement
- Combat metaphors discouraged ("destroy," "demolish," "own," etc.)
- Celebrate collaborative inquiry more than competitive success
- Model humility and genuine curiosity

**6. Balance Recognition**
- If debate winners are recognized, also recognize:
  - Best questions asked
  - Most thoughtful synthesis
  - Most constructive bridge-building
  - Best clarification of complex concepts
  - Most helpful newcomer support
- Diverse forms of contribution valued equally

### Community Norms and Moderation

**Explicit Norms:**

✅ **Encouraged:**
- Collaborative exploration
- Changing your mind when you learn something
- Acknowledging good points from others
- Asking genuine questions
- Respectful disagreement

❌ **Discouraged:**
- Treating philosophy as combat
- Seeking to "defeat" other users
- Status-seeking through debate victories
- Dismissing non-competitive engagement as "not real philosophy"
- Gatekeeping based on argumentation skill

**Moderator Training:**
- Watch for debate culture creep
- Intervene when competitive dynamics harm inclusive conversation
- Protect users who prefer exploratory dialogue
- Balance competitive and collaborative spaces

**Cultural Messaging:**
Regular community updates that reinforce:
- Philosophy is for everyone, not just skilled debaters
- Questions are as valuable as answers
- Changing your mind is intellectual growth, not weakness
- Multiple ways to engage philosophically are valid
- Debate is one tool, not the only or best approach

## Debate as Learning Tool (Not Status Game)

### Reframing Competitive Debate

**From:** Proving you're smart, winning points, building reputation

**To:** Developing skills, testing ideas, learning through structured feedback

### Educational Value of Debate

**What Debate Mode Can Teach:**

1. **Argument Construction**
   - Structuring reasoning clearly
   - Supporting claims with evidence
   - Logical validity and soundness
   - Anticipating objections

2. **Critical Analysis**
   - Identifying assumptions
   - Spotting logical fallacies
   - Evaluating strength of evidence
   - Finding weaknesses in reasoning

3. **Intellectual Rigor**
   - Precision in language
   - Consistency across arguments
   - Thorough treatment of objections
   - Philosophical sophistication

4. **Persuasive Communication**
   - Clarity of expression
   - Effective rhetoric
   - Organizing complex ideas
   - Adapting to audience

5. **Resilience and Growth**
   - Receiving critical feedback productively
   - Strengthening weak arguments
   - Learning from losses
   - Iterative improvement

**These are valuable skills!** The question is how to teach them without creating toxic competitive culture.

### Debate for Improvement, Not Supremacy

**Good Debate Culture:**

✅ **Post-Debate Reflection**
- "What did I learn from this?"
- "How can I strengthen my reasoning?"
- "What feedback was most valuable?"
- Focus on personal growth

✅ **Collaborative Improvement**
- Debate partners help each other get better
- Shared interest in strong arguments on both sides
- "Iron sharpens iron" rather than "destroy the opponent"

✅ **Process Over Outcome**
- Celebrating good reasoning regardless of win/loss
- Recognizing when you lost to a better argument as learning success
- Valuing the practice itself

✅ **Skill Progression**
- Track your own improvement over time
- See how your arguments get stronger
- Celebrate development, not just victories

❌ **Bad Debate Culture:**

❌ Tracking win/loss records publicly
❌ Debate rankings and leaderboards
❌ "Undefeated champion" status-seeking
❌ Using victories to claim intellectual superiority
❌ Treating losses as humiliation

**Design Principle:** Make debate outcomes visible to participants (for learning) but not broadcast to whole community (to prevent status games)

### Debate Tournaments as Community Events

**If Platform Hosts Tournaments:**

**Good Approach:**
- Voluntary participation
- Emphasis on fun and learning
- Prizes for various categories (not just "winner")
  - Most Improved
  - Best Sportsmanship
  - Most Creative Argument
  - Best Use of Historical Philosophy
- Celebrate all participants
- Friendly, inclusive atmosphere

**Bad Approach:**
- Mandatory participation
- High stakes (significant rewards/recognition)
- Only winner celebrated
- Exclusionary (only "good" debaters welcome)
- Serious, high-pressure atmosphere

**Goal:** Tournaments as community-building fun events, not serious competitions that create status hierarchies

## Recommendations

### Core Design Principles

1. **Conversation First, Debate Optional**
   - Default to collaborative mode always
   - Debate is explicitly opt-in
   - No pressure to engage in competitive evaluation

2. **Transparent Judging**
   - Criteria clear before debate begins
   - Detailed feedback explaining decisions
   - Multiple judge options available
   - Acknowledge subjectivity limitations

3. **Learning-Focused**
   - Frame debate as skill development tool
   - Emphasize growth over winning
   - Provide actionable feedback for improvement
   - Celebrate learning from losses

4. **Cultural Protection**
   - Actively prevent debate culture dominance
   - Limit visibility of competitive achievements
   - Promote diverse forms of engagement
   - Moderate against combat metaphors and status-seeking

5. **User Agency**
   - Easy to opt out of debate entirely
   - Can't be forced into debate mode
   - Filter options to hide debate content
   - Customizable judging criteria

### Specific Feature Recommendations

**Phase 1 (MVP) - Intentionally Minimal:**
1. **Conversation mode only** - Don't launch with debate mode
2. **Establish collaborative culture first**
3. **Build community around inquiry and exploration**
4. **Debate mode added only after cultural foundation is solid**

**Rationale:** Platform culture is set early. Start with your core values (collaborative inquiry), add competitive features later only if there's demand and culture is strong enough to handle it.

**Phase 2 (If/When Debate Added):**
5. **Simple opt-in debate mode** - Clear toggle, both parties agree
6. **Transparent criteria** - Users see evaluation framework
7. **Basic AI judging** - Criteria-based scoring
8. **Detailed feedback** - Learning-focused, actionable

**Phase 3 (Advanced Debate Features):**
9. **Multiple judge types** - AI, peer, panel options
10. **Tournament system** - Optional community events
11. **Debate coaching** - AI help strengthening arguments
12. **Advanced analytics** - Track skill development over time

**Possibly Never:**
- Public leaderboards
- Debate rankings visible to all
- Win/loss records on profiles
- "Top Debater" status symbols
- Gamification of competitive success

**Rationale:** These create status hierarchies that undermine inclusive philosophical community

## Implementation Priority

### Phase 1: Foundation (Months 1-6)

**DO:**
- Build conversation mode with AI facilitation
- Establish culture of collaborative inquiry
- Create welcoming environment for diverse participation
- Develop community norms around respectful dialogue

**DON'T:**
- Launch debate mode yet
- Promise competitive features
- Create any status hierarchies

**Rationale:** Cultural foundation must be solid before introducing competitive elements. Many successful platforms regret adding competitive features too early.

### Phase 2: Testing the Waters (Months 7-9)

**IF there's demand:**
- Small-scale debate pilot with select users
- Very clear labeling as experimental feature
- Gather extensive feedback
- Monitor cultural impact closely

**Decision point:** Does debate mode enhance platform or threaten culture?
- If enhancing: Expand gradually with safeguards
- If threatening: Keep minimal or remove
- Culture preservation is higher priority than feature completeness

### Phase 3: Thoughtful Expansion (Months 10-12+)

**IF pilot successful:**
- Expand debate mode availability
- Add more sophisticated judging
- Create optional tournament events
- Continue monitoring cultural impact

**Continuous vigilance:**
- Regular culture checks
- Survey users about competitive vs. collaborative balance
- Adjust features based on community health
- Be willing to roll back features if they harm culture

### Alternative: Separate Platform

**Consider:** Debate mode might work better as entirely separate platform
- Main platform: Philosophical dialogue and exploration
- Separate platform: Competitive philosophical debate
- Different communities, different norms
- No cultural bleed-over

**Advantages:**
- Crystal clear separation
- Each platform optimized for its purpose
- No culture wars within single community

**Disadvantages:**
- More complex to build and maintain
- Splits user base
- Less cross-pollination

**Worth considering** if debate culture threatens main platform mission

## Success Metrics

### Cultural Health Metrics (Most Important)

**Primary Indicators:**
1. **Inclusive Participation**
   - Diverse participation continues after debate mode introduced
   - Newcomer retention rates stable or improving
   - No decrease in exploratory discussions
   - **Target:** Maintain >75% of discussions in conversation mode

2. **Respectful Engagement**
   - Community norms of respect maintained
   - Low incidence of hostile interactions
   - Collaborative language usage stable
   - **Target:** <5% of discussions flagged for hostile tone

3. **User Satisfaction**
   - Users feel platform is welcoming and inclusive
   - Low reports of feeling intimidated or excluded
   - High satisfaction with community culture
   - **Target:** >80% agree "platform welcomes diverse engagement styles"

### Debate Mode Utility Metrics

**For Users Who Choose Debate:**

1. **Learning Outcomes**
   - Self-reported skill development
   - Improvement in argument quality over time
   - Value of feedback received
   - **Target:** >75% report debate mode helped them improve

2. **Appropriate Use**
   - Debate mode used for stated purposes (skill development, testing arguments)
   - Not used for harassment or status-seeking
   - Voluntary participation only
   - **Target:** <10% of debate mode reports are negative

3. **Engagement Quality**
   - High completion rate of debates
   - Positive feedback exchanged post-debate
   - Thoughtful engagement with criteria
   - **Target:** >80% completion rate, >70% positive experience ratings

### Anti-Metrics (Warning Signs)

**Critical Red Flags:**

1. **Culture Shift**
   - If conversation mode usage drops significantly
   - If combat language becomes common
   - If users report feeling pressured to debate
   - **Red Flag:** >50% of discussions in debate mode

2. **Exclusion Signals**
   - Newcomer participation drops
   - Diverse voices decrease
   - Users report feeling inadequate or intimidated
   - **Red Flag:** Significant drops in newcomer engagement

3. **Status Hierarchy**
   - Debate winners treated as superior
   - Gatekeeping based on debate success
   - Community divided into "good" and "bad" philosophers
   - **Red Flag:** Emergence of status-based cliques

4. **Hostile Environment**
   - Increase in reported harassment
   - "Debate me!" challenges as attacks
   - Gloating over victories, mocking losses
   - **Red Flag:** Rising reports of negative interactions

**Response to Red Flags:**
If any of these emerge, **debate mode should be restricted or removed** - cultural health is more important than feature completeness.

### Measurement Approach

**Quantitative:**
- Track conversation vs. debate mode usage
- Monitor newcomer retention rates
- Analyze language patterns (collaborative vs. combative)
- Measure reported harassment incidents

**Qualitative:**
- Regular culture surveys
- User interviews about experience
- Moderator observations
- Community feedback forums

**Decision Framework:**
- Monthly culture health reviews
- Quarterly decision: expand, maintain, or restrict debate mode
- Annual evaluation: keep, modify significantly, or remove
- **Bias toward caution:** When in doubt, protect culture over adding features

---

## Conclusion

Competitive debate has legitimate value for skill development and argument testing. However, it poses real risks to collaborative philosophical inquiry culture. The key is treating debate as an **optional, specialized tool** - clearly separated from the platform's core mission of inclusive, exploratory philosophical dialogue.

**The Paradox:** The better your collaborative conversation features, the less you may need debate mode. If AI facilitation, Socratic questioning, and context provision create deep, rigorous philosophical dialogue, many users may never want competitive evaluation.

**The Test:** Would the platform be excellent without debate mode? If yes, you can add it safely as optional advanced feature. If no, you're over-relying on competition to drive engagement - dangerous foundation.

**The Recommendation:** Start without debate mode. Build exceptional collaborative dialogue. Add debate only if there's clear demand and cultural foundation is strong enough to handle it. Keep it minimal, optional, and carefully monitored. Be willing to remove it if it threatens platform culture.

**Success looks like:** A platform where philosophical inquiry thrives in many forms - conversation, debate, question-asking, bridge-building, and more - but where competition is one tool among many, not the measure of philosophical worth.

The goal is philosophy for all, not philosophy as combat. Debate can serve that goal, but only if implemented with great care and clear subordination to the broader mission of collaborative inquiry.
